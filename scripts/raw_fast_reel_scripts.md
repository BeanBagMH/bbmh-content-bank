# BBMh — Fast Reel Format System
### 4 short-form formats · 20–32 seconds · Top of funnel · High frequency

---

## THE SYSTEM AT A GLANCE

| Format | Duration | Production Time | What It Does |
|--------|----------|-----------------|--------------|
| The Mirror | 20–28s | 30 min | Shows viewer their own reality plainly |
| The Question | 15–22s | 20 min | Asks one thing they can't immediately answer |
| The Contrast | 18–28s | 45 min | Pure visual — two worlds, one frame |
| The Frame | 10–18s | 15 min | One line of copy, trending audio, nothing else |

These four formats do different emotional jobs. Together they create a top-of-funnel that feels varied, not repetitive. The deep reels educate. These four create the felt gap that makes someone want the education.

---

## FORMAT 1 — THE MIRROR

**What it is:**
One specific, uncomfortable observation about the viewer's reality. No case study. No framework. No setup. Just a true thing said plainly by someone who has seen it enough times to say it without hedging.

**Why it works:**
The viewer recognises themselves. Recognition creates more trust faster than any argument. When someone thinks "that's me" — they follow. When they think "how did they know" — they save it.

**Structure:**
```
[0–3s]   The observation — stated directly, no warm-up
[3–18s]  One implication — what this costs or means, one sentence only
[18–25s] One beat of silence or hold — let it land
[25–28s] Optional: one soft close line, or just the BBMh logo
```

**Production:**
Single talking head shot. One take, one angle. No b-roll needed. No graphics. Trending audio or ambient. Can be filmed anywhere that looks intentional — the studio, a cafe, even a car. Edited in under 30 minutes.

**The rule:**
No case study. No "like Apple did" or "like Paper Boat." Just the observation. That's what makes it fast. That's also what makes it feel real.

---

### THE MIRROR — 12 READY SCRIPTS

**M-01**
Most B2B businesses have spent years getting the product right and about three days getting the brand right. The price negotiation shows it every time.
*[pause]*
The product was never the problem.

**M-02**
There's a version of your business that has been live on the internet for years. You've never seen it the way a first-time buyer does. That view is the one that counts.

**M-03**
Your best salesperson has a deck, a pitch, a follow-up sequence. Your brand — the thing that runs ahead of your salesperson into every room — has none of that. It just shows up and says whatever it says.

**M-04**
The business that wins the deal isn't always the best. It's the one that looked most ready to deliver before anyone checked.
*[pause]*
That's not unfair. That's just how buyers manage risk.

**M-05**
You'd notice immediately if your factory floor looked like your competitor's. You've probably never noticed that your website does.

**M-06**
Your oldest client doesn't know what your website looks like. Your newest prospect made a decision based on nothing else.
*[pause]*
Those two things are connected.

**M-07**
Most business owners can describe in detail what they make. Almost none of them can finish this sentence cleanly: we are this, for this person, and here's why only we do it this way.
*[pause]*
That's not a product problem. That's a brand problem.

**M-08**
You spent six months getting the product right. Your competitor spent six months getting the brand right. The buyer can't tell the difference between the two until they've already chosen one of you.

**M-09**
A buyer who finds you through a referral already trusts you. A buyer who finds you through Google trusts nothing. Your website is the only difference between those two conversations — and most businesses have written it for the first person, not the second.

**M-10**
Premium pricing isn't something you decide at the quoting stage. It's built — or not built — long before the buyer asks for a number.

**M-11**
Most Indian family businesses are running on a brand that was designed for the market they were in ten years ago. The market changed. The brand didn't get the message.

**M-12**
The brand inconsistency that buyers notice isn't the big stuff. It's the three slightly different logos. The two different fonts on the same proposal. The website colour that doesn't match the visiting card. Small things. That together say: this company doesn't have its house in order.

## FORMAT 2 — THE QUESTION

**What it is:**
One question. Asked directly. No answer given. The viewer sits with it.

**Why it works:**
Unanswered questions create cognitive itch. The viewer either comments with their answer, saves the reel to think about later, or — if the question landed — goes and does something about it. All three outcomes are wins.

**Structure:**
```
[0–5s]   Context-setter if needed — one line maximum, or cut straight to the question
[5–12s]  The question — delivered slowly, with weight
[12–18s] Hold — silence, ambient audio, or the question stays on screen
[18–22s] BBMh logo or end card — nothing else
```

**Production:**
Even simpler than The Mirror. This can be text on screen with no voiceover at all — just the question, the BBMh palette, trending audio, held for 18 seconds. Or it can be a direct-to-camera delivery where you ask and then go quiet. Either version takes under 20 minutes.

**The rule:**
One question only. If you ask two, neither lands. The power is in the single, specific, slightly uncomfortable question that the viewer can't immediately and confidently answer.

---

### THE QUESTION — 12 READY SCRIPTS

**Q-01**
When did you last open your own website on your phone as if you'd never heard of the company?

**Q-02**
If a buyer who had never heard of you Googled your company name right now — what would they find? And would it be enough?

**Q-03**
Does your logo work in black and white? Have you checked?

**Q-04**
Can you describe what your brand communicates in one sentence — without mentioning the product?

**Q-05**
Has your brand evolved since your business did?

**Q-06**
Your best client would recommend you enthusiastically. Would the brand they find when they look you up hold up to that recommendation?

**Q-07**
If you sent your own visiting card to the client you most want to win — would you feel proud or slightly apologetic about it?

**Q-08**
What does your email signature say about your company to someone who has never heard of you?

**Q-09**
Is the first thing a buyer finds about your business better or worse than the first thing they'd find about your best competitor?

**Q-10**
You know exactly what makes your product better than the competition. Does your brand communicate any of it before the conversation starts?

**Q-11**
If you had to describe your ideal client and they had to describe their ideal vendor — would each description fit the other?

**Q-12**
The last deal you lost on price — are you certain it was actually about price?

## FORMAT 3 — THE CONTRAST

**What it is:**
Pure visual. Two versions of the same thing — two websites, two visiting cards, two logos, two email signatures, two trade fair stalls, two proposal covers. Side by side or cut between. Minimal or no voiceover. One line of text does all the work.

**Why it works:**
Visual contrast is processed before conscious thought. The viewer's brain has already formed an opinion before the text appears. The text just names what the brain already felt. This format requires no arguments — the visual makes the argument instantly.

**Structure:**
```
[0–3s]   Version A — the weaker version, presented neutrally (no explanation)
[3–8s]   Version B — the stronger version, presented neutrally
[8–15s]  The text line — one sentence that names what you're seeing
[15–22s] Hold — let the comparison sit
[22–28s] BBMh logo
```

**Variations:**
- Split screen (both versions on screen simultaneously)
- Cut between (Version A, then Version B)
- Scroll through (mobile screen scroll — bad site, then good site)
- Zoom in (one detail that tells the whole story)

**Production:**
This is where BBMh's design skills are the production advantage. Every contrast concept below can be produced entirely in design software BBMh already uses. No shoot needed. No talent needed. No location needed. Takes 30–45 minutes to produce. Batch five in a day if the concepts are prepared.

**The rule:**
Never label which version is "good" or "bad." Show both neutrally. The text line names the observation — never the verdict. The viewer draws the verdict themselves. This keeps it non-preachy and keeps the engagement high.

---

### THE CONTRAST — 12 READY CONCEPTS

**C-01 — The Visiting Card**
Show: Two visiting cards, same industry (manufacturing, export, textile — pick your BBMh client archetype)
Version A: Generic font, crowded, clip-art logo, no visual hierarchy
Version B: Clean, weighted, considered typography, strong mark
Text line: "Both people left the same meeting. One got called back."

**C-02 — The Email Signature**
Show: Two email signature screenshots
Version A: Plain text, Gmail address, no logo, no designation clarity
Version B: Logo, company domain, clean layout, one visual accent
Text line: "This is the first thing they see before they read your email."

**C-03 — The Logo at Scale**
Show: Same logo (or two comparable logos) at three sizes — WhatsApp DP, visiting card, banner
Version A: Breaks at small size, illegible at large, inconsistent weights
Version B: Holds at all three sizes, readable, intentional
Text line: "A logo needs to work here, here, and here. Most work at only one."

**C-04 — The Website on Mobile**
Show: Screen recording — same type of business, two different mobile websites
Version A: Misaligned, requires zooming, contact button hard to reach
Version B: Clean, fast loading, clear CTA, works with a thumb
Text line: "This is how most buyers see you first."

**C-05 — The Proposal Cover**
Show: Two proposal cover pages
Version A: Word document, clip-art border, inconsistent fonts, no visual hierarchy
Version B: Designed PDF, brand colours, considered layout, company logo properly placed
Text line: "Before they read a single word."

**C-06 — The Trade Fair Stall**
Show: Two trade fair or exhibition stall setups (can be illustrated or reference images)
Version A: Mismatched banners, too much text, no clear visual identity
Version B: Unified brand across every piece, clean messaging, designed materials
Text line: "Same industry. Same product. Different shortlist."

**C-07 — The Catalog Cover**
Show: Two product catalog covers for the same industry
Version A: Text-heavy, photo quality inconsistent, no design grid
Version B: Editorial photography, considered layout, brand consistent
Text line: "The buyer doesn't know your product yet. This is all they have."

**C-08 — The Google Search Result**
Show: Two Google search result screenshots for two similar business names
Version A: No website, no Google Business listing, nothing but a phone directory entry
Version B: Clean website snippet, Google Business listing, reviews, some presence
Text line: "This is the due diligence. 90 seconds. Already decided."

**C-09 — The Three Logo Problem**
Show: Three slightly different versions of "the same" logo — different weights, different colours, different files used across different materials
Text line: "Which one is the real you? Your buyers are asking the same question."

**C-10 — The Brand Before/After**
Show: BBMh actual client work — before the rebrand and after
No labels. No explanation. Just the transformation.
Text line: "Same business. Different conversation."
*(This one is BBMh's portfolio in motion. Strong CTA post.)*

**C-11 — The Font Problem**
Show: Same company name written in five different fonts across five different touchpoints (card, website header, brochure, proposal, social bio)
Text line: "Consistency is a trust signal. This is what inconsistency looks like to a buyer."

**C-12 — The Homepage First Impression**
Show: Two homepages — first fold only, no scrolling
Version A: Generic hero image, vague headline like "Solutions for Your Business"
Version B: Specific headline, clear offering, visual identity immediately readable
Text line: "8 seconds. This is the entire decision."

## FORMAT 4 — THE FRAME

**What it is:**
One sentence. On screen. Trending audio. BBMh palette. Nothing else.

**Why it works:**
Saves and shares. This is the format people screenshot and send to their business partner. The sentence has to earn that behaviour — it needs to be the kind of observation that makes someone think: I need my co-founder/MD/partner to see this.

**Structure:**
```
[0–2s]   Blank screen or BBMh logo — brief hold before text appears
[2–12s]  The sentence — appears in full or builds word by word
[12–18s] Hold — sentence stays on screen
[18s]    BBMh logo
```

**Production:**
Literally typography, colour, audio, logo. Design it in Canva or After Effects. Batch 10 of these in under two hours. No shoot. No talent. No location. The copy is the entire product.

**The rule:**
The sentence cannot be generic. It cannot be the kind of thing that could come from any branding account. It must be specific to the BBMh lens — brand, visual identity, business reality for Indian founders. If it reads like something a marketing podcast would say, rewrite it.

**Typography note:**
Use the BBMh palette intentionally. Dark background #252527, text in white or yellow #FAD74F for the key word or phrase. Or reverse — yellow background, dark text. The visual should feel BBMh before the copy is even read.

## THE POSTING SYSTEM

This is how the four formats work together across a week.

**Suggested cadence — 5 posts per week:**

| Day | Format | Type |
|-----|--------|------|
| Monday | The Mirror | Top of funnel — observation |
| Tuesday | The Frame | Wide reach — shareable |
| Wednesday | Deep Reel | Authority — existing bank |
| Thursday | The Contrast | Visual — discovery |
| Friday | The Question | Engagement — felt gap |

**Every two weeks:**
Replace one deep reel slot with an Indian Brand or Translation Stack script from the existing bank. This maintains depth in the feed without requiring new research.

**Monthly batch production:**
One half-day session produces: 8 Mirror scripts (filmed together), 6 Contrast visuals (designed together), 10 Frame copy cards (batch-designed). That's roughly 24 pieces of fast content produced in one session — enough for six weeks of top-of-funnel posting.
